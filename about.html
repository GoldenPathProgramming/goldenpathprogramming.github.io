About the Golden Path of Game Programming

The Golden Path is not the easiest way to make games, nor the fastest. In fact, it is a long, difficult path to follow. There are many ways to develop games, but the Golden Path will teach you mastery and skills which will serve you in all your programming endeavours in the future, whether you find yourself using a game engine, writing web apps, or developing embedded firmware. The Golden Path leads to game development, but along the way, you will develop yourself.

The Modern Approach

Most budding game developers are advised: "Pick an engine and just start making games!" Though some succeed with this approach, many struggle to piece together their ideas in an engine they don't understand, and blame themselves when they run out of motivation. Burning out in this way is not a flaw in your character; it's the natural result of striving for something without the skills required to achieve it. While a game engine seems to simplify game development by providing prebuilt functionality, engines in fact are usually more complicated than the systems they seek to simplify. After weeks, months, even years of struggling to create your game, you may be unable to progress, or struggle with unexpected behaviour. Look deeper into the backgrounds of those who succeed in this way, and, while there certainly are some prodigies, many of these successes had careers in software development or other experience which enabled them to grapple with engines much more competently than most beginners can.

The Old Way

Many skilled, successful developers were kids when the home computer revolution began. They started on a Commodore 64, Apple II, or ZX Spectrum. These computers came with very limited software and presented programming as part of the day-to-day usage of a computer. The C64 booted straight to a BASIC programming prompt! Those kids' first experiences with computers taught them that, with a few simple commands, they could program their computers. This was surely a thrilling realization, and with user manuals, magazines and books filled with pages of code examples to type in, these kids naturally learned how to implement all kinds of functionality. As they grew their understanding, they sought to bring their own ideas to life by writing original code. When their aspirations grew, they hit the limits of what these basic languages were capable of, so they delved into the internal details of their computers with memory poking/peeking and assembly language. Those nerdy enough to dig into the technical details became computer experts in their teens, and either got jobs at budding software companies or created tech demos at home. As higher-level systems developed, like the C programming language, C++, specialized engines (like id Tech 1, Build, Source), and generalized engines (like Unity, Unreal, Godot), they were empowered by these capabilities, while maintaining full knowledge of the underlying systems. With their deep understanding, they are able to utilize the power and convenience offered by high-level tools, without ever finding themselves unable to solve a problem, or unsure of how to implement their idea. And if that's how John Carmack, John Romero, Tim Cain, Sean Barret, Linus Torvalds, and many more, got their start, why not follow their lead?

My Path

When I was 10 years old, I googled, "How to make games." One of the first results was, naturally, GameMaker. I downloaded the engine, followed some "getting started" guides and had a character moving on the screen in no time. I was hooked! Over the next few years I played at game development just like any other hobby, making tiny implementations of simple ideas. My first complete game was a math quiz I made for my high school math class, called, "Math Genius." This artifact is unfortunately lost to time. While I was able to get some things done in GameMaker, I never understood how the engine really worked, and when I attempted to implement my ideas, they often didn't work as I expected. When I tried to architect anything larger than tiny demos, they became messes too difficult to continue to work on.

I became interested in lower-level programming, and discovered C++. I made a "Hello world" program and was so excited to be starting from what I imagined was the ground level, working my way back up to making a full, graphical game. I found Lazy Foo, Nehe, VideoTutorialsRock, and plenty more. However, after opening a window with SDL and rendering polygons with OpenGL, I still felt lost. How could I get from here to architecting a full game? And while I'd traded the big black box of GameMaker for a few smaller black boxes, I still didn't understand what was going on under the hood. I struggled at programming at a relatively low-level in C++, with a dirth of guidance, and when I became too frustrated to continue I dabbled in other engines like Unity, never feeling like I really knew what I was doing or had a clear path forward.

In 2015, at age 21, I was fed up with having never released a complete game, so I decided to make the smallest game I could come up with. On September 21st, I released, "It All Revolves Around You," written in C++, using SFML, OpenAL, sfeMovie, etc. I unfortunately no longer have the source code, but I assure you, it was a mess. While I implemented the gameplay myself, I still relied on black boxes to do the serious stuff. It felt good to finally release a finished game, but I still didn't feel capable of making anything more complex. However, I was commited to figuring out game development, so my struggle continued. From 2015 to 2018 I released 1 game per year, made with C++, PICO-8, and Godot, all while dabbling in other engines and languages.

All throughout these experiences, I was struggling to somehow become a better programmer, watching videos, following tutorials, reading books. Handmade Hero showed me that it's possible to write a game from scratch, although that project didn't become exactly what I needed it to be. Even so, I decided to try to write all the code to make a game from scratch in C, without using a library like SDL. In 2019, thanks to André LaMothe's book, I made a 3D, software rasterized maze game. using the low-level code I had written for all the platform stuff (on Linux anyway - I used SDL for Windows support). This was the first time I really felt I could become a decent programmer, understand the system from the ground up and be empowered to write whatever code I wanted. I still had a lot to learn, and despite most people having a ton of free time during COVID, I unfortunately became much busier. Between 2020-2024, I plugged away in my spare time, focused just on getting better at programming and writing things from scratch. Through 2024 and most of 2025, I finally felt I'd hit my stride - I did some professional contract programming work and my from-scratch code libraries were really coming together.

I created a small game called GrapeStomper Rescue, mainly to make sure my framework had everything necessary to make a full game. I released it on Steam as game #1 of Nick's Saturday Morning Games for Windows, Linux and MacOS. The only libraries used were the platform native APIs (Win32+WASAPI, Xlib+Pulse, Cocoa+AudioUnit) and OpenGL just for putting the pixels on the screen and vsync. I wrote a game in C, just about as from-scratch as is reasonable, shipped on Steam to (a few dozen) paying customers and (to my surprise) it worked! I got one report of it not working on someone's Steam Deck, and several reports from people surprised by how smoothly everything ran! It was at this point, I finally had become a decent programmer, with the confidence that I am good enough to create my games from the ground up, ship on multiple platforms and write code good enough to sell. And if I run into any problems, I'm confident I'll be able to fix them. I also made a music creation app from scratch, writing my own UI library (which is awful, but a good learning experience). Then, I created NSMG #2, Transistor Glow - a small electronics puzzle game - in just 2 months. I finally figured out how to program games.

What a massive bloody waste of time! I would not recommend the above path to anyone! I strove and struggled to learn how to program games for 20 years! When I became interested in low-level programming at 14 years old or so, if I had found a reasonable path to writing games from scratch, I would have surely released something like GrapeStomper Rescue in just a couple of years, and been as capable as I am now by 17 instead of 32. Unfortunately, that pathway didn't exist then, and it still doesn't. Handmade Hero is the only major attempt at providing a full, free education on how to program games from scratch, and I have a huge amount of respect for Casey Muratori for making the attempt. He didn't fully succeed, but he did show us that it's possible, and gave us a starting point. He also showed that there are many of us who desperately want to make games, but, for one reason or another, can't simply, "pick an engine and start making games." That's why I'm making The Golden Path. I'm creating the clear and complete path, from opening a window all the way to shipping a commercial game, all programmed from scratch, that 14-year-old Nick needed, but never found. I'm not the most qualified to do so, but Carmack, Romero, Cain, Barret, Blow and all the rest are busy making games, or doing other things with their lives. I don't have any big, successful, games, nor even many shipped games, but I have written a game framework from scratch in C, and shipped multiple commercial games with it, so I feel I'm at least qualified to show you how to write a game from scratch.

The Golden Path

Getting a Commodore 64 (or emulating one), dabbling in BASIC, then moving on to assembly, is a noble idea. There's certainly something to be said for simply doing things the way your elders did them. And, if that's what you want to do, I say: Go for it! It'll be fun, you'll learn a ton, and I'm sure you'll end up a better programmer. The Golden Path does not begin with an 80s home computer, however. The Golden Path is the guide I wish I had when I first started getting serious about game programming. The Golden Path, for now, assumes you've found a way to learn some C programming yourself, and will show you how to open a window, handle user input, produce video and audio, write logging, data serialization, and more, on Windows, Linux, and MacOS. It will show you how to combine all that code into a platform-independent framework for building games, with a multithreaded architecture, and it'll show you how to write a simple game in it. Then, it'll show you how to write a more advanced game, including adding new, more advanced architecture to the framework. Every step of the path will be clear, and while you may choose to ignore a platform you're not interested in, skip parts you don't need, or divert from the Path at some point, the Path will exist to clearly demonstrate every step needed to start from scratch, all the way to shipping your completed games. And if you do leave the Path early, most of what the Path has taught you will remain useful to you in other programming and computing endeavours. When you reach the end of the Path, not only will you be capable of programming your games from scratch; with much greater understanding of the underlying technology, you'll also be more capable of making games in engines, should that be your Path forward.

I look forward to seeing what you make...

Why C?

When you learn to make games in an engine or very high-level language/framework, you learn mostly how to use that specific tool. The C programming language is one of these tools, but it is a relatively thin layer above the system, and most other programming languages, frameworks, and engines, inherit significantly from C. When you learn to do something in C, you learn foundational knowledge which will continue to be useful whether you choose to remain at this low level, or go all the way up to making games in a fully-featured engine.
